## Who's writing (read this before editing anything)

Joe is a **native Portuguese speaker who writes and publishes in English**. This drives the whole editing job, and it cuts two ways:

1. **Under-polish:** ESL phrasing, Portuguese-influenced word order, article/preposition slips, and stiff constructions a native engineer wouldn't write. Fix these — the text should read as natural, fluent English.
2. **Over-polish:** the "fix" that sands his personality off into generic AI prose. This is the worse failure. Do NOT trade his voice for smoothness.

**The goal: natural, native-fluent English that still unmistakably sounds like Joe.** Grammar and flow are the priority. Everything below is about keeping it his.

## What "sounds like Joe" is

- Short, direct declaratives. Concrete over abstract. He'd rather show the thing than praise it.
- Honest, first-person engineering war stories: what broke, what he changed his mind about, what he'd redo.
- Dry humor and mild self-deprecation. He undercuts hype rather than adding it.
- Names real tools, versions, dates, and numbers.
- Uses field vocabulary precisely (YARA-L, Chronicle, OCSF, nftables, honeytoken) — never dumbed down.
- His own emoji, added by hand. **Leave them exactly as they are. Never add, remove, relocate, or comment on emoji.**

### This is drift — he wrote these, but they don't sound like him

Inflated ESL-formal register (Latinate padding, thesaurus reach):

> "Maintaining a high level of confidence in CSIRT analysis is **paramount**." → "Keeping CSIRT's confidence high matters."
> "This **conundrum**, which I term the..." / "I am **keenly focused** on..." / "Before **delving into** the dilemma..."

AI hype register (enthusiasm inflation — his single most common tell):

> "What followed was **mesmerizing**." / "The efficiency gains are **staggering**." / "**Unprecedented velocity**." / "I am **incredibly excited** for what comes next." / "would help me accelerate Lantana **big time**." / "This **supercharged** existing tools." / "it proved to be a **game-changer**."

These are the two ways his writing slips when it stops sounding like him: toward stiff formality (fix by simplifying to plain words) and toward hype (fix by stating the fact and cutting the adjective — a number beats a superlative).
